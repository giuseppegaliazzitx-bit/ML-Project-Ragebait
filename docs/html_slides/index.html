Motivation

What is rage bait?

Content intentionally crafted to provoke anger, frustration, or outrage from readers.

  • Uses polarized, inflammatory, or bad-faith statements to guarantee a reaction.
  • Often values response volume over truth, nuance, or constructive discussion.
  • Can look similar to ordinary controversy, profanity, or genuine outrage.
The core challenge is intent: not every angry post is rage bait, and not every offensive word has the same social meaning.
MotivationDefinition
Speaker notes

Give a plain-language definition first. Emphasize that the project is not trying to ban disagreement or detect every emotional post. It focuses on content designed to farm negative reactions.

Negative engagement economy

The incentive problem

Modern feeds often reward interaction volume, even when the interaction is anger.

1. PostBad-faith or inflammatory content is published.
2. ReactionUsers reply, quote, argue, and amplify.
3. RankingAlgorithms interpret activity as relevance.
4. RewardThe content gains visibility and the pattern repeats.

Algorithmic incentives

Engagement-based ranking can elevate polarizing content.

Engagement farming

Manufactured negativity becomes a visibility strategy.

Social cost

Discourse quality drops and bad actors become more visible.

Modeling ladder

Three levels of representation

The experiments test whether richer text representations produce meaningful gains.

Tier 1: TF-IDF

Logistic Regression and Linear SVC over unigram and bigram features.

Tier 2: FFNN

Train-only embeddings, mean pooling, hidden layers, ReLU, and dropout.

Tier 3: BERT

Fine-tuned bert-base-uncased with contextual token representations.

TF-IDF max features: 20,000 FFNN vocab: 10,975 Binary BERT max length: 80 Multiclass BERT max length: 128
MethodologyBaselines first, transformer last

Final findings

What we learned

The final system is stronger because its evaluation target is human judgment.

Baselines matter

TF-IDF models are strong, especially for binary detection.

FFNN is limited

Mean-pooled embeddings add little and can underperform linear models on macro F1.

BERT is best

Contextual modeling improves aggregate scores and every class-wise F1.

The remaining challenge is semantic overlap at the Trolling, Derogatory, and Hate Speech boundaries.
ConclusionModeling implications
Speaker notes

Summarize in three claims. Classical baselines are essential, shallow neural models are not automatically better, and BERT earns its compute cost on the hardest semantic distinctions.
